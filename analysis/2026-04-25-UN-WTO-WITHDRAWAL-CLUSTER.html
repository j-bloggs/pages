What Drove This
January 2026 — The Trigger: Trump signed an executive order withdrawing the US from 66 international organisations, including 31 UN sub-bodies (WHO, UNHRC, IPCC, etc.). This created legitimate "full charter exit next?" fear and drove the baseline from near-zero historical to 18–20%. Context: the US has now formally left more UN-affiliated bodies than at any time since 1945.
February 2026 — Moderating Signal (Partly Missed): Trump signed a bipartisan spending bill that preserved near-full US dues to the UN core body. Signals the administration distinguishes between sub-body exits (politically viable) and charter-level UN withdrawal (legally complex, diplomatically catastrophic). This capped the initial spike at 18–20%.
April 13–25 — Accumulation Phase: Market drifted from 23% toward 45% as isolationist narrative reinforced (Iran crisis, tariff escalation, NATO burden-sharing tensions). No single catalyst identified; likely slow accumulation on the "what's next after 66 orgs?" thesis.
Tonight's −13pp Reversal — Waltz Testimony: Ambassador Mike Waltz testified before the Senate Foreign Relations Committee that the Trump administration has made "real strides in reforming" the UN — not leaving it. The stated posture is conditional engagement ("if the US is the biggest funder, the UN must work for American interests"), not charter exit. Combined with the February funding bill, this signals sub-body exits have satisfied the nationalist base; full UN withdrawal would need a much larger trigger. WTO market (37%) did not have a comparable reversal, suggesting trade-related withdrawal risk is viewed as structurally higher than the UN.
Macro Implications for Australian Investors

🔴 WTO at 37% — Trade Policy Tail Risk

US WTO withdrawal = US exits MFN (most-favoured-nation) obligations. American tariffs on all trading partners — including Australia — would no longer be bound by WTO schedules. While AUSFTA (bilateral treaty) provides some protection on direct AUS-US trade, the systemic effect on global supply chains is severe. ASX: resources/materials exporters face demand uncertainty; industrials and manufacturers negative. AUD likely under pressure as global trade confidence falls.

🔴 IMF at 14% — Monetary Stability Tail

US IMF withdrawal would remove the largest single quota holder. USD role in SDR basket, IMF conditionality frameworks, and EM crisis backstops all at risk. Long-run USD credibility implications (negative for USD strength, positive for gold). Australian external financing costs impacted through global risk-off channel.

🟡 UN at 33% — Governance Signal

US UNSC veto loss would fracture global security governance. Historically unprecedented. Near-term market impact would be severe but hard to price in advance. Note: the Waltz testimony tonight materially reduces the probability of this happening before 2029. At 33%, the market still prices significant residual risk — but the trajectory has reversed.

🟡 World Bank at 21% — EM Development

US withdrawal from the World Bank would reduce AUS export demand from developing economies that depend on World Bank-funded projects (infrastructure, agriculture). Indirect but real channel for ASX commodity exporters targeting EM markets.
