Cross-Market Analysis
🔑 The New Structural Story: Iran-Managed Hormuz
Ghalibaf's statement crystallises what the IRGC veto implied: Iran is not reopening Hormuz — Iran is reorganising Hormuz under Iranian management. The difference matters for markets:

• Pre-crisis assumption: Hormuz reopening = free-transit regime returns, WTI normalises toward $65-68 (supply glut + de-escalation premium removed).
• Post-Ghalibaf reality: Hormuz reopening = Iranian route authority + transit fees (post-60-day waiver) + exclusive corridor assignment. Insurance War Risk premiums may remain elevated even after physical mine clearance completes, because route authority isn't settled by clearing mines.

This implies a higher structural floor for WTI/Brent than the pre-crisis baseline. Even in a "successful" July 31 resolution scenario, the post-war Hormuz is not the pre-war Hormuz. The pre-crisis WTI of $73-75 may now represent the new "fully reopened" equilibrium (vs $65-68 if straight return to pre-war). For Australian petrol: A$1.90-2.00/L may be the structural floor in the resolution scenario, not A$1.75-1.80/L.
Switzerland Talks: Better Than "Paused" Framing
Last night's email described talks as "paused." The fuller picture: Round 1 technical talks concluded successfully on June 23 ("agreement on arrangements for future negotiations, including working groups and implementation mechanisms" — Al Jazeera confirmed). VP Vance called it a "good foundation." The IRGC-Oman corridor dispute on June 25 disrupted the scheduling of Round 2 — Pakistan says technical level resumes Tuesday June 30. This is "Round 2 briefly delayed," not "talks collapsed." The route authority dispute is part of the Switzerland agenda, not a reason to abandon it.
