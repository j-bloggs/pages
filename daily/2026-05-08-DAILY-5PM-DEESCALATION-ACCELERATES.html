Analysis — De-escalation Reasserts Through Geopolitical Noise

The defining story of Friday May 8 is oil market confidence in the de-escalation thesis surviving its most serious one-day test yet. This morning's US strikes on Qeshm Island and Bandar Abbas — coming hours after Iran parliament speaker Ghalibaf dubbed the Axios MOU "Operation Fauxios" — created the most credible escalation catalyst in weeks. The market's response: WTI $110 May fell an additional −10pp (53.5% → 43.5%), and Iran invasion hit a new all-time session low of 20.5%.

The catalyst for the continued de-escalation trade is the CNBC report confirming Iran has become the primary agenda item for Trump–Xi summit (May 14–15, Beijing). US Treasury Secretary Bessent publicly confirmed Iran will feature prominently. This reframes the summit not just as a trade/tariff event but as a joint US-China pressure campaign on Tehran — China (Iran's primary oil buyer) providing diplomatic leverage, US providing the military/sanctions framework. Market interpretation: China will not let Iran kill the summit by refusing the MOU; Beijing will apply pressure before May 14.

Cross-market coherence check (69th bifurcation): WTI falling ✓ | Invasion at new low ✓ | Direct meeting May 31 recovering to 48.5% ✓ | Nuclear deal resilient at 61.5% ✓ | Hormuz June 30 stable at 72.5% ✓ | No contradictions. The Qeshm strikes are being read as tactical pressure before the MOU window closes (Tehran response due by ~May 9 per 48h window opened May 7), not as ceasefire collapse.

One notable intra-day shift: Hormuz May 31 drifted lower to 40.5% (−3pp, −8.5pp/24h) while June 30 held at 72.5%. The 32pp May31/June30 spread is widening, not compressing — market explicitly pulling the Hormuz opening timeline to June (post-summit implementation window), not May (pre-summit). This is the summit-framework scenario playing out: deal announced May 14–15 → 2–4 week implementation → Hormuz lifts late May/early June.

WTI spot context: WTI at ~$94–95/bbl intraday (Brent proxy ~$100–105). Still well above pre-war levels but -$15 from blockade peak. If MOU is accepted and Hormuz opening confirmed, spot WTI has a direct path to $85–88 (WTI $85 May probability was at 66% yesterday, pricing 2-in-3 chance of intraday $85 touch this month — consistent with current trajectory). Australian petrol currently A$2.10–2.25/L; de-escalation scenario points to A$1.95–2.10/L by June.

Summit Watch — T-7 Days to Beijing

CNBC (May 8): "Iran focus at Trump-Xi summit may delay progress on tariffs, rare earths." US Treasury Secretary Bessent confirmed Iran and Hormuz will be the primary agenda items in Beijing May 14–15. This is a structural reframing: the summit is now primarily a geopolitical/energy event (Iran MOU framework + Hormuz opening pathway), with tariff negotiations as secondary.

Macro implication: China has a direct incentive to produce an Iran outcome at the summit — Beijing is paying $18-20/bbl Brent premium for Iranian oil diverted through COSCO tankers (MOFCOM Announcement No.21, May 4). Hormuz opening eliminates that premium and allows China to re-enter global crude markets openly. This aligns China's economic interests with the de-escalation trade. Expect China to apply significant pressure on Tehran in the days before May 14.

Risk to summit: If Iran rejects the MOU before May 14, the summit loses its centerpiece and may produce only a tariff/trade framework. In that scenario, expect: Hormuz June 30 retreats 5–8pp, WTI rebounds, and Iran invasion increases 2–4pp. The 95.6% Trump-China market prices summit happening, not a successful Iran outcome — so even a "failed Iran outcome" summit leaves the 95.6% intact.
