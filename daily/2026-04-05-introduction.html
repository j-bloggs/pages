What This Is

This is an automated intelligence report that monitors Polymarket — the world's largest prediction market — and translates its signals into what they mean for financial markets, with a focus on Australia.

On Polymarket, people bet real money on the outcomes of future events. A market reading "99.7% Yes" means the aggregate of all bettors — weighted by capital committed — estimates a 99.7% chance the event will happen. Markets with tens of millions in volume represent serious conviction, not casual opinion. Because bettors lose money when they're wrong, these prices tend to reflect genuine probability estimates faster and more honestly than polls or pundit forecasts.

We track roughly 69 markets across six categories, selected because their outcomes have plausible transmission mechanisms to asset prices, central bank decisions, and trade flows:

  • Geopolitics Conflicts, regime change, territorial control — currently dominated by the US-Iran theatre and Gulf state involvement.
  • Monetary Policy US Federal Reserve rate decisions, hike/cut probabilities. The US Fed sets the global cost of money; every central bank responds to it.
  • Commodities Oil price targets, supply infrastructure (Kharg Island, Strait of Hormuz), and actions that affect production or shipping.
  • Trade & Diplomacy US-China relations, state visits, tariff signals. Australia's economy is deeply coupled to China.
  • Crypto Bitcoin and major token price levels — useful as a risk-sentiment gauge.
  • Macro Signals Recession and stagflation indicators. We also watch for markets that don't exist yet — their creation is itself a signal.

Why Australia

Australia sits in a uniquely exposed position: a developed, Western-aligned economy with deep commodity export dependence on China and near-total import dependence for refined fuel. This means global shocks hit Australia from multiple directions simultaneously.

Key Exposure Points

  • Fuel imports (~90%): Any sustained oil shock flows directly into petrol, freight, and CPI with minimal domestic buffer.
  • China trade: Australia's largest partner by a wide margin. Iron ore, coal, LNG all flow north. US-China friction hits Australian exporters first.
  • RBA positioning: The RBA watches the US Fed closely. When the US Fed is paralysed, the RBA's options narrow too.
  • AUD as a risk currency: In conflict-driven commodity spikes (stagflationary, not growth-driven), the AUD tends to weaken against USD despite high commodity prices.
  • Trade route proximity: Australian LNG exports transit near contested Gulf shipping corridors.

Each report maps transmission chains from prediction market events to Australian instruments: AUD/USD, ASX 200, RBA rate expectations, and key sectors.

Australia Impact Assessment

  • Fuel & CPI: WTI at $130+ translates to petrol well above $2.50/L, possibly approaching $3.00/L. Feeds directly into CPI via transport, logistics, and food costs. The RBA's inflation fight gets harder.
  • RBA rate path: Same trap as the US Fed — oil inflation argues for tightening, but growth is slowing. Expect the RBA to hold and jawbone rather than act.
  • AUD vulnerability: A conflict-driven oil spike is stagflationary, not growth-driven. If risk-off intensifies, AUD/USD weakens despite high commodity prices as capital flows to USD safety.
  • ASX sectors: Energy stocks (Woodside, Santos) benefit. Banks and consumer discretionary get hit. Iron ore miners face mixed signals — China demand uncertainty versus supply tightness.
  • LNG trade routes: Australian exports transit near contested waterways. Broader Gulf conflict could paradoxically constrain Australia's own energy exports — higher prices but potentially reduced volume.
  • China-US friction: Fading Trump-China visit (65.0%, down 6.5pp) signals ongoing trade tension. Iron ore and coal demand directly exposed.
