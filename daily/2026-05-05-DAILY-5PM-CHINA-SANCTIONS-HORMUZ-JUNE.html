2. China Sanctions Defiance — Summit Stakes Now Existential

⚠ MOFCOM Announcement No.21 (May 4): China Orders Firms to Ignore US Sanctions on Iranian Oil

China's Ministry of Commerce issued a directive explicitly ordering Chinese citizens, companies, and organisations to "shall not recognize, enforce, or comply with" US Executive Orders sanctioning Iranian oil traders. The trigger: US Treasury designated five Chinese refiners on April 24 (Hengli Petrochemical and four smaller Shandong/Hebei teapots) for buying Iranian crude.

This is unprecedented — China had quietly allowed its largest firms to comply with US Iran sanctions since 2018 to protect access to the US financial system. Saturday's announcement signals a fundamentally more aggressive posture.

What this means for the summit: The May 14-15 Beijing summit now has a new centrepiece dispute — China's firms openly buying sanctioned Iranian oil. Trump cannot ignore this without appearing weak on Iran sanctions; Xi cannot back down without appearing to yield to US "bullying" (per China's UN envoy framing). The summit is now an all-or-nothing diplomatic moment: if Trump and Xi can build a grand bargain (tariff rollback + sanctions framework + Hormuz timeline), June resolution of Hormuz becomes plausible. If talks fail on sanctions, oil pressure intensifies.

Trump-China market response: 82.0% (02:15 AEST overnight low) → 91.5% (5PM today). The +9.5pp intraday recovery shows the market reads China's sanctions defiance as pre-summit leverage, not cancellation intent. Cancellation triggers (↓70%, ↓65%) remain 21-27pp away.

Paradox: China continuing to buy Iranian oil at scale actually reduces the effectiveness of US-Iran sanctions — Iran gets hard currency from China regardless. This could either reduce Iran's incentive to negotiate (less economic pressure) or give Trump something to concede at the summit (sanctions relief as part of a deal) that enables Hormuz opening. The market appears to be pricing the latter scenario via Hormuz June 30 at 55%.
